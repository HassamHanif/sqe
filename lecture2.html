Why Do We Need Quality Models?

Section 1 — Slide 4

Without a quality model, "quality" is just subjective opinion. Quality models turn abstract ideas into concrete, measurable criteria. Six key purposes:

PurposeExplanation
Shared VocabularyGives all stakeholders (devs, testers, managers, clients) a common language so "maintainable" has a precise agreed meaning.
MeasurabilityBreaks abstract quality into quantifiable attributes and metrics. Deming's principle: "You can only improve what you measure."
PrioritisationNot all attributes matter equally in every context. A pacemaker needs Reliability > Performance; a social media app needs Usability > Portability.
Standards ComplianceProvides basis for ISO certification, regulatory audits, and contractual quality obligations.
Process ImprovementIdentifies gaps between current quality and targets, guiding investment in test automation, CI/CD, and code reviews.
Risk ReductionAriane 5 (1996): software integer overflow → USD 370M loss. Northeast Blackout (2003): software alarm failure → USD 6B cost, 55M people affected.
💡 Exam Tip: Be ready to state at least 3 purposes of quality models and explain each. The real-world failure examples (Ariane 5, Northeast Blackout) may appear in scenario questions.

McCall's Quality Model (1977)

Sections 2 — Slides 5–6
Origin & Purpose

Proposed by Jim McCall, Paul Richards & Gene Walters (1977), commissioned by the US Air Force Electronic Systems Division. One of the first systematic attempts to define software quality as measurable attributes.

Reference: McCall et al. (1977). Factors in Software Quality, Vols 1–3. NTIS AD/A-049 014. US Air Force.

Structure: 3 Product Perspectives → 11 Quality Factors → Criteria → Metrics

🖥️ Product Operation

How well it runs — does it perform correctly and safely for the end user?

Correctness Reliability Efficiency Integrity Usability

🔧 Product Revision

How well it changes — can developers easily correct, adapt, or test it?

Maintainability Flexibility Testability

🔄 Product Transition

How well it adapts — can it move to new environments or be reused?

Portability Reusability Interoperability

The 11 Quality Factors — Full Definitions:

FactorPerspectiveDefinitionExample Criteria
CorrectnessOperationDegree to which software meets its specifications and fulfils user objectives.Traceability, Consistency, Completeness
ReliabilityOperationProbability of failure-free operation over a specified time period in a specified environment.Accuracy, Error Tolerance, Consistency
EfficiencyOperationAmount of computing resources and code required to perform a function.Execution Efficiency, Storage Efficiency
IntegrityOperationDegree to which access to software and data can be controlled — preventing unauthorised use.Access Control, Access Audit
UsabilityOperationEffort required to learn, operate, prepare input, and interpret output.Operability, Training, Communicativeness
MaintainabilityRevisionEffort required to locate and fix an error in an operational program.Consistency, Simplicity, Conciseness, Self-documentation
FlexibilityRevisionEffort required to modify an operational program.Modularity, Generality, Expandability
TestabilityRevisionEffort required to test a program to ensure it performs its intended functions.Simplicity, Instrumentation, Self-documentation
PortabilityTransitionEffort required to transfer the program from one hardware/OS environment to another.Machine Independence, SW Independence
ReusabilityTransitionExtent to which software can be reused in another application.Generality, Modularity, Self-documentation
InteroperabilityTransitionEffort required to couple one system to another.Modularity, Communications Commonality, Data Commonality

The Factor–Criteria–Metric (FCM) Hierarchy — McCall's Core Contribution:

Level 1 — Factor
High-level quality attribute visible to users & managers. Qualitative — cannot be measured directly.

Example: Reliability
→
Level 2 — Criteria
Software properties that contribute to a factor. Engineer-level definitions.

Example: Accuracy, Error Tolerance, Consistency
→
Level 3 — Metric
Quantitative measure of a criterion. Actual numbers collected during dev/testing.

Example: MTBF, Defect Density, Failure Rate
Worked Example — FCM for Reliability

A payment gateway tested over 1,000 hours experiences 4 failures. MTBF = 1000÷4 = 250 hours. Defect density = 2.5 defects per KLOC. These metrics operationalise the Reliability factor.

⚠️ Exam Tip: Know all 11 factors and which perspective they belong to. Commonly tested: "Which factors are in Product Operation?" (Correctness, Reliability, Efficiency, Integrity, Usability). Also know that FCM = Factor → Criteria → Metric (3 levels, in order). Limitation: overlapping factors (e.g., Maintainability vs. Flexibility), and it's dated (1977) — doesn't cover security-by-design, cloud scalability, etc.

Boehm's Quality Model (1978)

Section 3 — Slide 7
Origin & Key Innovation

Published by Barry Boehm, John Brown & colleagues (1978). Key shift from McCall: quality is modelled as a hierarchical weighted tree, not a flat factor list. Numeric weights enable quantitative quality scoring and trade-off analysis.

Reference: Boehm et al. (1978). Characteristics of Software Quality. North-Holland Publishing Company.

Model Structure (top to bottom):

LevelNodeDescription
RootGeneral UtilityTop-level goal: Can the software be used as-is and will it continue to work well over time?
Primary CharacteristicsPortability, Reliability, Efficiency, Human Engineering (Usability), Testability, Understandability, ModifiabilityMajor quality dimensions — each has a numeric weight assigned.
Intermediate NodesE.g., Reliability → Accuracy, Self-ConsistencyFurther decompose primary characteristics into sub-qualities.
Primitive ConstructsStructured design, self-descriptiveness, legibility, augmentabilityLeaf nodes — directly measurable properties.

Key Feature: Weighted Nodes

What weights enable:

  • Calculate an overall quality score as a weighted sum across the tree
  • Perform trade-off analysis (e.g., improve maintainability at cost of efficiency)
  • Model the cost-quality relationship during development

Limitations:

  • Weight assignment is subjective — who decides each weight?
  • Complex to apply in real industrial projects
  • Designed in 1978 — doesn't address modern concerns (cloud, AI, APIs)

McCall vs. Boehm — Comparison:

DimensionMcCall (1977)Boehm (1978)
StructureFlat — 3 perspectives, 11 factorsHierarchical weighted tree
MeasurementFactor → Criteria → Metric (3 levels)Weighted nodes from root to primitives
QuantificationMetrics at the lowest level onlyNumeric weights at EVERY node
Trade-off AnalysisLimited — factors are independentBuilt-in — weights enable trade-offs
Primary AudienceDoD programme managersResearchers and cost modellers
LimitationOverlapping factorsSubjective weights; complex to apply
💡 Key Distinction: McCall = flat structure, practical for government contracts. Boehm = hierarchical tree with weights, better for research and cost-quality modelling. Both are dated and don't address modern security-by-design, cloud scalability, or AI quality concerns.

FURPS / FURPS+ Model (1987 / 1990s)

Section 4 — Slide 8
Origin

Developed by Robert Grady at Hewlett-Packard (1987). Designed as a practical, lightweight framework for defining and tracking quality requirements in industrial software projects. FURPS+ extended by Rational Software in the 1990s.

Reference: Grady & Caswell (1987). Software Metrics: Establishing a Company-Wide Program. Prentice-Hall.
F

U
Usability
R
Reliability
P
Performance
S

CategoryDefinitionSub-attributes / Examples
F — FunctionalityWhat the system does — core feature set, capabilities, and security controls.Feature set, Capabilities, Generality, Security (access control, encryption, authentication)
U — UsabilityHow easily end-users can learn and operate the system.Human factors, Aesthetics, UI Consistency, Documentation quality, Accessibility (WCAG)
R — ReliabilityProbability of failure-free operation; ability to recover from failures.Frequency/severity of failure, Recoverability, Predictability, Accuracy, MTTF, MTTR
P — PerformanceHow fast and efficiently the system performs under various load conditions.Response time, Throughput (TPS), Resource utilisation (CPU, RAM), Scalability
S — SupportabilityHow easily the system can be tested, extended, configured, and maintained after deployment.Testability, Extensibility, Adaptability, Maintainability, Configurability, Serviceability

FURPS+ Extensions — 4 Constraint Categories:

Design Constraints

Restrictions on HOW the system must be designed. E.g., must use microservice architecture; must conform to REST API conventions.

Implementation Constraints

Restrictions on coding standards, languages, or tools. E.g., must use Python 3.11; must follow OWASP Secure Coding Guidelines.

Interface Constraints

Restrictions on external system interactions. E.g., must integrate with SAP ERP via SOAP; must support OAuth 2.0.

Physical Constraints

Hardware requirements or limitations. E.g., must run on minimum 2 GB RAM; must not exceed 50 MB installation footprint.

⚠️ Exam Tip: Know what each FURPS letter stands for and its definition. "Supportability" is the one people forget — it includes testability, extensibility, maintainability, and configurability. Also know the 4 FURPS+ constraint categories and give an example for each. Best suited for: Agile, industrial software projects (HP context).

Product Quality vs. Process Quality

Section 6 — Slides 11–13

📦 Product Quality

  • Focuses on the delivered software artefact
  • Assessed via metrics, reviews, and testing
  • What the customer directly experiences
  • Defined by ISO 25010 characteristics
  • Metrics: defect density, MTTF, usability scores
  • Measured at delivery and during operation

⚙️ Process Quality

  • Focuses on HOW the software is developed
  • Assessed via process audits and maturity models
  • Affects product quality indirectly
  • Defined by CMMI, ISO 9001, ISO/IEC 15504 (SPICE)
  • Metrics: review rates, rework effort, test coverage
  • Measured throughout the entire SDLC

Three Critical Principles (must memorise):

Principle 1 — Process influences product: Good processes reduce defect insertion rate, improve traceability, and enable faster correction. High process maturity makes high product quality more likely.
Principle 2 — Process does NOT guarantee product: You can have an ISO 9001-certified process and still produce a product that fails user needs — if requirements were wrong or architecture was flawed. NASA Mars Climate Orbiter (1999): CMMI Level 5 process → still lost due to a metric units mismatch between two teams. This illustrates Principle 2.
Principle 3 — Low process quality ALMOST ALWAYS causes low product quality: Ad-hoc, chaotic processes (CMMI Level 1) consistently produce defect-ridden, unreliable software. This is the strongest causal direction.

CMMI — Capability Maturity Model Integration (5 Levels):

5
Optimising
Continuous process improvement through quantitative feedback; defect prevention; technology innovation systematically adopted.
4
Quantitatively Managed
Processes measured and controlled using statistical techniques; predictable performance within defined limits.
3
Defined
Processes documented, standardised, and integrated enterprise-wide; an organisational standard process exists.
2
Managed
Basic project management in place; processes are planned, performed, monitored, and controlled on a per-project basis.
1
Initial
Ad-hoc and chaotic. No stable process environment. Success depends on individual heroics. Processes cannot be repeated reliably.
⚠️ Key Facts for Exam: As of 2024, fewer than 10% of organisations worldwide achieve CMMI Level 4 or 5. Most commercial organisations operate at Level 2–3. CMMI Level 3+ is mandatory for many defence, aerospace, and medical device software contracts.

Activity 1 — Recap

Quick Poll: Quality Attribute Identification

Identify the quality attribute violated in each scenario and state whether it is functional or non-functional.

Scenario A: A hospital record system crashes when more than 50 users are logged in simultaneously, causing data loss and unavailability.
✅ Model Answer

Attribute violated: Reliability (non-functional) — specifically the sub-characteristics of Fault Tolerance (system did not continue operating under load) and Availability (system became unavailable). Also potentially Performance Efficiency if the crash was caused by resource overload rather than a pure reliability defect.

Type: Non-functional — it describes HOW WELL the system operates, not WHAT it does.

Scenario B: An e-commerce website takes 12 seconds to process a payment, causing users to abandon checkout.
✅ Model Answer

Attribute violated: Performance Efficiency (non-functional) — specifically Time Behaviour (response time is unacceptably slow). In FURPS terms, this falls under 'P — Performance'. In McCall terms, this is the Efficiency factor (Product Operation perspective).

Type: Non-functional — describes how fast/efficiently the system processes the payment, not whether the payment feature exists.

Scenario C: A mobile banking app has a confusing interface where the "Transfer" and "Pay Bill" buttons look identical, causing frequent user errors.
✅ Model Answer

Attribute violated: Usability (non-functional) — specifically Operability and User Error Protection (ISO 25010 sub-characteristics). In FURPS: 'U — Usability'. In McCall: Usability factor (Product Operation perspective).

Type: Non-functional — the transfer/pay-bill features may exist and work correctly (functional), but the interface makes them hard to distinguish (non-functional quality failure).

Activity 2

FCM Mapping Exercise — Student Portal

For a Student Portal system (login, view grades, submit assignments, view timetable), apply McCall's FCM hierarchy.

Step 1: Identify the 3 most critical McCall factors for a Student Portal. Step 2: Propose 2 criteria per factor. Step 3: Suggest a measurable metric for each criterion.
✅ Model Answer

Factor 1: Reliability (Operation)
Criteria: Accuracy (grades displayed must be correct), Error Tolerance (system should not crash if server is slow)
Metrics: Defect density in grade calculation module (defects/KLOC); MTBF of the portal server (hours between failures)

Factor 2: Usability (Operation)
Criteria: Operability (students can submit assignments without needing a manual), Communicativeness (error messages are clear and helpful)
Metrics: Average task completion time for assignment submission (seconds); number of support tickets per 100 users per month

Factor 3: Maintainability (Revision)
Criteria: Simplicity (codebase is easy to understand and modify), Self-documentation (code is commented and documented)
Metrics: Average time to fix a reported bug (hours); Lines of documentation per 100 lines of code ratio

Classify each requirement into the correct FURPS or FURPS+ category.

Classify the following 8 requirements:
1. The app shall respond to a booking request in under 1.5 seconds.
2. The system shall encrypt all payment data using AES-256.
3. The driver onboarding flow shall be completable in under 10 minutes.

Activity 4

CMMI Level Classification

Classify each organisation vignette at the correct CMMI level (1–5).

Org A: "Each developer decides their own testing approach; no documentation standards exist. Projects succeed or fail based on individual effort."
Org B: "Each project has a documented test plan and schedule. The team tracks defects and holds weekly reviews, but processes differ across projects."
Org C: "All projects follow the same company-wide Software Development Process defined in our process library. All developers are trained on it."
Org D: "We collect defect density, test coverage, and cycle time data from all projects. We use statistical process control dashboards reviewed weekly."
Org E: "We use Six Sigma and process mining to identify bottlenecks in our SDLC and run controlled experiments to improve our development process quarterly."
✅ Model Answer

Org A → CMMI Level 1 (Initial) — Ad-hoc and chaotic. No stable process environment. Success depends on individual heroics.

Org B → CMMI Level 2 (Managed) — Basic project management in place; processes are planned, monitored, and controlled on a per-project basis, but not yet standardised across the organisation.

Org C → CMMI Level 3 (Defined) — Processes are documented, standardised, and integrated enterprise-wide. An organisational standard process exists and all staff are trained.

Org D → CMMI Level 4 (Quantitatively Managed) — Processes measured and controlled using statistical techniques. Predictable performance within defined limits.

Org E → CMMI Level 5 (Optimising) — Continuous process improvement through quantitative feedback; uses data-driven experiments to improve the development process systematically.

Activity 5 — Debate

McCall (Practical) vs. Boehm (Rigorous)

Group A argues McCall's FCM approach is more practical for industrial use. Group B argues Boehm's weighted tree is more rigorous. Write the argument for BOTH sides.
✅ Model Answer

Group A — McCall is more practical:
McCall's 3-perspective, 11-factor, FCM structure is easy to explain to managers and clients. It maps directly to contractual deliverables (especially in DoD projects). The three-level hierarchy (Factor → Criteria → Metric) provides a clear workflow for quality engineers. Any team can apply it without needing advanced statistical knowledge. It has been validated in real government software programmes since 1977.

Group B — Boehm is more rigorous:
Boehm's weighted tree allows numeric quality scoring across every node, enabling organisations to calculate an overall quality score and compare alternatives. It explicitly supports trade-off analysis — you can quantify what you sacrifice in efficiency to gain maintainability. The hierarchical decomposition down to primitive (directly measurable) constructs is more theoretically sound. In research and cost-modelling contexts, the quantitative rigour of Boehm's model is essential for defensible results.

Instructor summary: In practice, McCall is used more in government/defence contracts (mandated); Boehm is cited more in research papers. ISO 25010 has largely replaced both in modern commercial settings.

Activity 6 — Discussion

Best Model for Mobile App Development

Which quality model best suits mobile application development — considering rapid release cycles, diverse device constraints, app store compliance, and end-user experience expectations?
✅ Model Answer (Full Marks Response)

Recommended: FURPS+ as primary, ISO 25010 for compliance checks.

FURPS+ is most suitable because:
— Rapid release cycles align with FURPS's lightweight, user-story-friendly structure. Each sprint can be reviewed against F, U, R, P, S categories quickly.
— Diverse device constraints (RAM, screen sizes, OS versions) map directly to FURPS+ Physical and Implementation Constraints.
— End-user experience is directly addressed by U (Usability) and P (Performance) categories — both critical for app store ratings.
— App store requirements (e.g., WCAG accessibility for Apple App Store) fit the U — Usability category with WCAG sub-attribute.

ISO 25010 is also useful for:
— Security compliance (GDPR, data protection) — maps to the Security characteristic.
— Apps targeting regulated sectors (health, finance) requiring formal compliance certification.

McCall and Boehm are less suitable: McCall is designed for defence contracts and is overly formal for a startup or agile mobile team. Boehm's weighted tree is complex and best for research, not sprint-based delivery.

3 Explain the Factor–Criteria–Metric (FCM) hierarchy in McCall's model with one example. Recall ▼ Show
4 MarksCLO-1

The FCM hierarchy has three levels:

Factor (Level 1): A high-level qualitative quality attribute visible to users and managers. Cannot be measured directly. Example: Reliability.

Criteria (Level 2): Software properties that contribute to the factor, providing engineer-level definitions. Example: Accuracy, Error Tolerance, Consistency.

Metric (Level 3): A quantitative measure of a criterion — the actual numbers collected during development or testing. Example: MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures), Defect Density (defects/KLOC), Failure Rate.

Worked example: A payment gateway tested over 1,000 hours has 4 failures → MTBF = 250 hours; Defect Density = 2.5/KLOC. These metrics operationalise the Reliability factor.

4 What is the key structural difference between McCall's model and Boehm's model? Recall ▼ Show
3 MarksCLO-1

McCall's model has a flat structure — 3 perspectives and 11 independent quality factors, measured via a three-level Factor–Criteria–Metric hierarchy. It assigns metrics only at the lowest level.

Boehm's model has a hierarchical weighted tree structure, where quality decomposes from a root node (General Utility) through primary characteristics down to primitive constructs. Crucially, Boehm assigns numeric weights at every node in the tree, enabling quantitative quality scoring, overall quality calculation, and trade-off analysis between characteristics. This is Boehm's primary contribution over McCall.

5 MarksCLO-1

U — Usability: How easily end-users can learn and operate the system.
R — Reliability: The probability of failure-free operation over time and the system's ability to recover from failures.
P — Performance: How fast and efficiently the system performs its functions under various load conditions.
S — Supportability: How easily the system can be tested, extended, configured, and maintained after deployment.

🔬 Section B — Application / Comparison Questions

9 Differentiate between Product Quality and Process Quality. Explain the three principles that describe their relationship, using an example for each. Apply ▼ Show
6 MarksCLO-1

Product Quality focuses on the delivered software artefact — what the customer directly experiences. It is assessed via metrics, testing, and reviews. Governed by ISO 25010. Measured at delivery and during operation.

Process Quality focuses on how the software is developed — the development process itself. Assessed via process audits and maturity models (CMMI, ISO 9001). Affects product quality indirectly. Measured throughout the SDLC.

Principle 1 — Process influences product: Good processes reduce defect insertion and enable faster correction. Example: A team following rigorous code review and CI/CD processes will have fewer bugs reaching production, improving product reliability.

Principle 2 — Process does NOT guarantee product: A perfect process can still produce a defective product if requirements are wrong. Example: NASA's Mars Climate Orbiter (1999) was built under CMMI Level 5 processes but was lost due to a metric units mismatch — a requirements/integration error, not a process failure.

Principle 3 — Low process quality ALMOST ALWAYS causes low product quality: Chaotic, ad-hoc processes (CMMI Level 1) consistently produce defect-ridden software. Example: A startup with no code reviews, no documentation, and no test plans will almost certainly ship a buggy, unmaintainable product.

10 Compare McCall's model and FURPS in terms of: structure, measurability, and best use context. Which would you recommend for an Agile mobile app team and why? Apply ▼ Show
6 MarksCLO-1

Structure: McCall uses 3 perspectives containing 11 quality factors with a 3-level FCM hierarchy. FURPS uses a flat structure with 5 categories and 4 optional constraint types (+).

Measurability: McCall reaches quantitative metrics at the leaf level (Metric level of FCM). FURPS combines qualitative and quantitative approaches — less prescriptive about metrics but more flexible.

Best use context: McCall → US government/defence contracts (mandated). FURPS → Agile industrial software, HP-style product development.

Recommendation for Agile mobile app team: FURPS+ — because its flat, category-based structure maps naturally onto user stories and acceptance criteria. Each sprint backlog item can be tagged as F/U/R/P/S. The FURPS+ constraints handle device and platform requirements (Physical/Implementation constraints), which are especially common in mobile development. McCall is too formal, too defence-oriented, and its FCM hierarchy is not designed for iterative delivery cycles.

🎭 Section C — Scenario-Based Questions

11 SCENARIO: A healthcare startup is building a patient monitoring mobile app. The client says: "The app must alert nurses within 3 seconds of an abnormal heart rate. It must work on Samsung Galaxy A-series phones. It must be developed in Flutter. It must recover automatically if the server drops." Apply FURPS+ to classify each requirement. Then state which McCall factors are most critical for this system and why. Scenario ▼ Show
10 MarksCLO-1

FURPS+ Classification:
— "Alert nurses within 3 seconds" → P — Performance (time behaviour / response time)
— "Work on Samsung Galaxy A-series phones" → FURPS+ Physical Constraint (specific hardware device requirement)
— "Developed in Flutter" → FURPS+ Implementation Constraint (technology/language specification)
— "Recover automatically if server drops" → R — Reliability (recoverability, fault tolerance sub-attributes)

Most critical McCall factors for this healthcare system:

1. Reliability (Operation): Most critical — patient monitoring is a safety-critical system. Any failure to detect or report abnormal heart rate could be life-threatening. Metrics: MTBF, failure rate under normal operating conditions.

2. Correctness (Operation): The system must accurately detect and classify heart rate anomalies — incorrect alerts (false negatives or false positives) are clinically dangerous. Metrics: Defect density in alert logic module.

3. Integrity (Operation): Patient health data is highly sensitive. Unauthorised access to heart rate data violates HIPAA/privacy laws. Access Control and Access Audit criteria are critical. Metrics: Number of unauthorized access attempts detected and blocked.

These three Operation perspective factors are prioritised because patient safety and data protection are more critical than, say, Portability or Reusability for this system.

12 SCENARIO: A software company wants to improve its quality. Currently, each project manager decides their own processes — there are no company-wide standards, and documentation is optional. Two consultants give advice: Consultant A recommends "implement ISO 25010 for all projects." Consultant B recommends "first achieve CMMI Level 3, then worry about ISO 25010." Who is correct, and why? Use the Product vs. Process Quality framework. Scenario ▼ Show
8 MarksCLO-1

Consultant B is more correct for this company's situation.

Current state analysis: The company is at CMMI Level 1 (Initial) — each project manager decides their own processes, no company-wide standards, documentation is optional, success depends on individual heroics.

Why Consultant B is right — Principle 3: Low process quality almost always causes low product quality. With CMMI Level 1 processes, even if engineers know the ISO 25010 characteristics, they have no consistent method to measure, control, or improve them. ISO 25010 defines WHAT to measure; CMMI defines HOW to build a reliable process to actually achieve those measurements.

Why Consultant A is incomplete: ISO 25010 is a product quality standard — it tells you what characteristics good software should have. But if the development process is chaotic and inconsistent (Level 1), you cannot reliably produce software that meets those characteristics. Measuring defect density means nothing if there is no standard process for reviewing or fixing defects.

Recommended approach: First implement CMMI Level 2 (basic project management — tracking, monitoring, basic standards), then Level 3 (organisation-wide defined process), and simultaneously begin introducing ISO 25010 quality criteria as measurement targets within those processes. The lecture's "best for large enterprise" recommendation confirms: ISO 25010 + CMMI together is the comprehensive solution — but the CMMI foundation must come first for a Level 1 organisation.

Use this real-world case to explain: (a) which McCall factor was violated, (b) which CMMI principle this illustrates, and (c) which quality model would have been most appropriate to prevent this failure. (a) McCall Factor violated:
Primary: Reliability (Operation perspective) — the alarm system failed to operate correctly over time under critical conditions. Criteria: Error Tolerance (the system did not tolerate or handle the alarm failure gracefully) and Accuracy (the system did not produce correct outputs when needed).
Secondary: Maintainability (Revision perspective) — the lecture notes specifically say "a reliability and maintainability defect that a systematic quality model might have surfaced earlier," suggesting the system's alarms were difficult to analyse and diagnose.

(b) CMMI principle illustrated:
Principle 1 — Process influences product quality. If the power management system had been developed under higher process maturity (CMMI Level 3+), there would have been standardised alarm testing procedures, documented failure mode analysis, and structured code reviews. These process controls would likely have detected the alarm software defect before deployment, reducing the probability of this catastrophic failure.

(c) Most appropriate quality model to prevent this:
ISO 25010 combined with CMMI. ISO 25010 would have required explicit measurement of Reliability (sub-characteristics: fault tolerance, availability, recoverability) and Maintainability (analysability) for the alarm software. CMMI Level 3+ would have ensured a standardised, rigorous testing and review process was applied. This combination — product quality standards + process maturity — gives the highest probability of preventing such safety-critical defects.

Key Terms, Definitions & Quick Reference

Quality Model
A structured framework that turns abstract notions of software quality into concrete, measurable, and reproducible criteria for evaluation.
McCall's Model (1977)
3 perspectives (Operation, Revision, Transition), 11 quality factors, measured via the Factor–Criteria–Metric (FCM) hierarchy. Commissioned by US Air Force.
FCM Hierarchy
Factor (qualitative, user-visible) → Criteria (engineer-level properties) → Metric (quantitative measurements). McCall's key contribution.
Product Operation
McCall's first perspective: Correctness, Reliability, Efficiency, Integrity, Usability — how well the software runs for end users.
Product Revision
McCall's second perspective: Maintainability, Flexibility, Testability — how easily developers can change and test the software.
Product Transition
McCall's third perspective: Portability, Reusability, Interoperability — how well the software adapts to new environments or integrates with other systems.
Boehm's Model (1978)
Hierarchical weighted quality tree from root (General Utility) to primitive constructs. Enables quantitative quality scoring and trade-off analysis. By Barry Boehm et al.
General Utility
The root node of Boehm's quality tree — "Can the software be used as-is and will it continue to work well over time?"
Primitive Constructs
Leaf nodes in Boehm's model that are directly measurable: structured design, self-descriptiveness, legibility, augmentability.
FURPS
Functionality, Usability, Reliability, Performance, Supportability — HP's practical quality model by Robert Grady (1987).
FURPS+
FURPS extended with 4 constraint categories: Design, Implementation, Interface, and Physical constraints. Extended by Rational Software (1990s).
Supportability
The 'S' in FURPS: Testability, Extensibility, Adaptability, Maintainability, Configurability, Serviceability. Often confused with Maintainability.
Product Quality
Quality of the delivered software artefact — what the customer experiences. Defined by ISO 25010. Measured via metrics and testing.
Process Quality
Quality of how the software is developed — the development process itself. Defined by CMMI, ISO 9001. Affects product quality indirectly.
CMMI
Capability Maturity Model Integration — 5-level framework for measuring and improving software process quality. Level 1 (Initial) to Level 5 (Optimising).
MTBF
Mean Time Between Failures — reliability metric: total operating time ÷ number of failures. Used in McCall FCM metrics and FURPS Reliability.
KLOC
Kilo Lines of Code (1000 lines). Used in defect density metric: defects per KLOC. A McCall FCM Level 3 metric for Reliability.
Trade-off Analysis
Evaluating how improving one quality attribute affects another (e.g., improving security may reduce performance). Boehm's model explicitly supports this via weighted nodes.
